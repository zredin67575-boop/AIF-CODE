Saiph
  • Nickname: “The Hunter” / “The Saucepan”
  • Brightest star: Betelgeuse — an unstable red supergiant ~425 ly away, usually magnitude ~0.5 but known to dim dramatically
  • Location: Southern sky
  • Best months: October-March (peak in January)
  • How to find it: Look for three bright stars in a straight line (Orion's Belt), with four bright stars forming a large rectangle around them

Background & Mythology

Orion appears in Babylonian star catalogues from around 1200 BCE, where it represented a celestial shepherd or warrior. In Greek mythology, Orion was a giant hunter placed among the stars by Zeus. Ancient Egyptians associated Orion with Osiris, god of the afterlife, and the alignment of the pyramids at Giza is thought to reflect the Belt stars. In Indigenous Australian astronomy, Orion's Belt and Sword often represent three brothers or hunters carrying spears (Yolŋ), or ancestral figures tied to seasonal food cycles. In Māori astronomy, the Belt is known as Tautoru and marks seasonal change.

Astronomy

Orion contains several major stars: Betelgeuse (red supergiant), Rigel (blue supergiant ~860 ly, magnitude ~0.1), Bellatrix (blue giant ~250 ly), and Saiph (blue supergiant ~650 ly). The constellation lies along the Orion Arm of the Milky Way, making it rich in star‐forming regions.

Deep-Sky Objects

Orion contains some of the most significant deep-sky objects in the sky: the Orion Nebula (M42), a massive star-forming region visible to the naked eye; the Horsehead Nebula, a dark nebula silhouetted against bright gas; and Barnard's Loop, a huge arc of glowing hydrogen gas.

Cultural Significance

Orion appears in cultures worldwide — Greek, Egyptian, Norse, Hindu, Māori, and many Indigenous Australian traditions — often representing hunters, warriors, or ancestral beings.

  • Location: Near Libra and Sagittarius
  • Best months: May-August (peak in July)
  • How to find it: Look for two triangles connected by a curved line of stars forming the tail

Background & Mythology

Scorpius appears in Sumerian records as early as 2000 BCE. In Greek mythology, it represents the scorpion sent to kill Orion, explaining why the two constellations never appear in the sky at the same time. In Polynesian navigation, parts of Scorpius form the “Fishhook of Maui.” In Indigenous Australian astronomy, Scorpius is often seen as a crocodile, eagle, or ancestral figure depending on the Nation.

Astronomy

Key stars include Antares (red supergiant), Shaula (blue subgiant ~570 ly), and Sargas (yellow giant ~270 ly). The constellation lies along the Milky Way, making it rich in star fields.

Deep-Sky Objects

Notable objects include the Butterfly Cluster (M6) and the Ptolemy Cluster (M7), both bright open clusters easily visible in dark skies.

Cultural Significance

Scorpius is central in Greek, Polynesian, and Indigenous Australian sky traditions, often marking seasonal change and navigation.

  • Nickname: “The Archer”
  • Brightest star: Kaus Australis — a blue-white giant ~140 ly away, magnitude ~1.8
  • Location: Toward the densest part of the Milky Way
  • Best months: July-August
  • How to find it: Look for the “teapot” shape or a central rhombus with curved arms

Background & Mythology

Sagittarius was recorded by the Babylonians as the god Pabilsag, a centaur-like archer. In Greek mythology, it is associated with Crotus, the satyr companion of the Muses. In Māori astronomy, parts of Sagittarius form the bow of Tautoru's hunting figure. Indigenous Australian interpretations focus on the bright Milky Way region rather than the Western constellation shape.

Astronomy

Notable stars include Kaus Australis (blue-white giant) and Nunki (blue subgiant ~230 ly). Sagittarius lies toward the Galactic Centre, making it one of the richest regions of the sky.

Deep-Sky Objects

Significant objects include Sagittarius A*, the supermassive black hole at the centre of the Milky Way (~4 million solar masses), the Lagoon Nebula (M8), and the Trifid Nebula (M20).

Cultural Significance

Sagittarius is important in Greek, Babylonian, and Polynesian traditions, and its region of the sky is central to many Indigenous Australian stories about the Milky Way.

Crux

  • Nickname: “The Southern Cross”
  • Brightest star: Acrux — a blue-white multiple star system ~320 ly away, magnitude ~1.3
  • Location: Southern sky, near Centaurus
  • Best months: Visible most of the year; highest in autumn
  • How to find it: Look for a small cross shape; use the two bright pointer stars in Centaurus

Background & Mythology

Crux was recognised by Ancient Greek sailors but later fell below the horizon for Mediterranean observers due to precession. It was rediscovered by European navigators in the 1500s. In Indigenous Australian astronomy, Crux is deeply significant — appearing in stories from the Noongar, Kamilaroi, Yolŋ, and many other Nations. In Māori astronomy, it is known as Te Punga, the anchor of the great sky canoe.

Astronomy

Key stars include Acrux (blue-white multiple system), Mimosa (blue-white giant ~280 ly), and Gacrux (red giant ~88 ly). Crux lies along the Milky Way, making it rich in star fields.

Deep-Sky Objects

Significant objects include the Jewel Box Cluster (NGC 4755), one of the most colourful open clusters, and the Coalsack Nebula, a prominent dark nebula.

Cultural Significance

Crux is central to navigation in the Southern Hemisphere and appears in the flags of Australia, New Zealand, Papua New Guinea, and Brazil. It is one of the most important constellations in Indigenous Australian sky knowledge.

Centaurus

  • Nickname: “The Centaur”
  • Brightest star: Alpha Centauri — a triple star system 4.37 ly away, includes a Sun-like star and a red dwarf, magnitude ~0.0
  • Location: Southern sky, surrounds Crux
  • Best months: April-June
  • How to find it: Look for the two bright pointer stars near Crux

Background & Mythology

Centaurus was known to the Babylonians and later adopted by the Greeks, who associated it with Chiron, the wise centaur. In Polynesian navigation, its bright stars were used for open-ocean wayfinding. Indigenous Australian interpretations vary, often focusing on the bright pointer stars as part of larger Milky Way narratives.

Astronomy

Key stars include Alpha Centauri (closest star system to Earth) and Beta Centauri (Hadar), a blue-white giant ~390 ly away. The constellation spans a large region of the southern sky.

Deep-Sky Objects

Significant objects include Omega Centauri, the largest and brightest globular cluster in the Milky Way, and Centaurus A, a powerful radio galaxy with a distinctive dust lane.

Cultural Significance

Centaurus is important in Greek, Polynesian, and Indigenous Australian traditions, often tied to navigation and seasonal change.

Carina

  • Nickname: “The Keel of a Ship” (formerly part of Argo Navis)
  • Brightest star: Canopus — a white supergiant ~210 ly away, magnitude ~-0.7
  • Location: Southern sky, along the Milky Way
  • Best months: January-March
  • How to find it: Look for Canopus, a very bright slightly yellow-white star below Sirius

Background & Mythology

Carina was once part of the massive Greek constellation Argo Navis, representing Jason's ship. It was divided into Carina, Puppis, and Vela by astronomer Nicolas Lacaille in the 18th century. In Indigenous Australian astronomy, stars in this region often form part of the Emu in the Sky or other Milky Way figures.

Astronomy

Carina contains Canopus, the second-brightest star in the night sky. It also contains Eta Carinae, a highly unstable luminous blue variable star system undergoing massive eruptions.

Deep-Sky Objects

The Carina Nebula (NGC 3372) is one of the largest star‐forming regions in the Milky Way, containing the Keyhole Nebula and Eta Carinae. It is brighter and larger than the Orion Nebula.

Cultural Significance

Carina's stars appear in Greek, Polynesian, and Indigenous Australian traditions, often forming parts of larger sky figures rather than a single constellation.

Canis Major

  • Nickname: “The Greater Dog”
  • Brightest star: Sirius — a white main-sequence star ~8.6 ly away, magnitude −1.46, the brightest star in the night sky
  • Location: Below Orion
  • Best months: January-March
  • How to find it: Follow Orion's Belt downward to Sirius

Background & Mythology

Canis Major was recognised by the Greeks as one of Orion's hunting dogs. In Ancient Egypt, the heliacal rising of Sirius marked the flooding of the Nile and the start of the new year. In Polynesian navigation, Sirius (Takurua) is a major seasonal marker. In Indigenous Australian astronomy, Sirius appears in stories about seasonal change and ancestral beings.

Astronomy

Sirius is a binary system consisting of Sirius A (white star) and Sirius B (white dwarf). Other notable stars include Adhara (blue giant ~430 ly) and Wezen (yellow-white supergiant ~1,600 ly).

Deep-Sky Objects

Canis Major contains the Canis Major Overdensity, a debated dwarf galaxy currently merging with the Milky Way. It also hosts the open cluster Messier 41 (M41), visible to the naked eye, and the emission nebula NGC 2359 (Thor's Helmet Nebula).

Cultural Significance

Canis Major is significant in many cultures worldwide, symbolising loyalty, hunting, and seasonal cycles. It appears in Greek, Egyptian, Polynesian, and Indigenous Australian traditions, often linked to Orion and seasonal changes.

Taurus

  • Nickname: “The Bull” (Zodiac)
  • Brightest star: Aldebaran — an orange giant ~65 ly away, magnitude ~0.9
  • Location: Near Orion and the Pleiades
  • Best months: November-February
  • How to find it: Look for a V-shaped cluster of stars with bright orange Aldebaran at one end

Background & Mythology

Taurus is one of the oldest recorded constellations, appearing in Babylonian astronomy over 4,000 years ago. In Greek mythology, it represents Zeus disguised as a bull. In many cultures, Taurus was associated with strength, fertility, and agriculture.

Astronomy

Taurus contains Aldebaran, the Hyades Cluster, and the famous Pleiades star cluster. Although Aldebaran appears within the Hyades, it is actually much closer to Earth.

Deep-Sky Objects

Major objects include the Pleiades (M45), one of the nearest open clusters to Earth, and the Crab Nebula (M1), the remnant of a supernova observed in 1054 CE.

Cultural Significance

Taurus appears in Babylonian, Greek, Egyptian, Māori, and Indigenous Australian astronomy, often connected to seasonal change and farming cycles.

Gemini

  • Nickname: “The Twins” (Zodiac)
  • Brightest star: Pollux — an orange giant ~34 ly away, magnitude ~1.1
  • Location: Near Orion and Taurus
  • Best months: December-March
  • How to find it: Look for two bright stars close together representing the twins' heads

Background & Mythology

Gemini represents the twins Castor and Pollux from Greek mythology. Similar twin figures appear in Babylonian and Roman astronomy traditions.

Astronomy

The constellation contains the stars Pollux and Castor, though Pollux is brighter. Castor is actually a complex multiple star system.

Deep-Sky Objects

Gemini contains the open cluster Messier 35 (M35) and several planetary nebulae. Pollux also hosts an exoplanet roughly twice the mass of Jupiter.

Cultural Significance

Gemini has long symbolised partnership, duality, and brotherhood in many cultures around the world.

Leo

  • Nickname: “The Lion” (Zodiac)
  • Brightest star: Regulus — a blue-white multiple star system ~79 ly away, magnitude ~1.4
  • Location: Northern side of the zodiac belt
  • Best months: March-May
  • How to find it: Look for a backwards question mark shape forming the lion's mane

Background & Mythology

Leo represents the Nemean Lion defeated by Heracles in Greek mythology. It has been recognised since Mesopotamian times and was associated with kingship and power.

Astronomy

Regulus lies almost directly on the ecliptic, meaning the Moon and planets frequently pass nearby. Denebola marks the lion's tail.

Deep-Sky Objects

Leo contains many galaxies, including the Leo Triplet — a group of interacting spiral galaxies visible through telescopes.

Cultural Significance

Leo has symbolised royalty, courage, and strength in cultures throughout history.

  • Nickname: “The Maiden” (Zodiac)

  • Best months: April-June
  • How to find it: Follow the curve of the Big Dipper's handle to bright orange Arcturus, then continue to Spica

Virgo has been linked to harvest goddesses in many ancient cultures, including Demeter and Astraea in Greek mythology.

Astronomy

Virgo is the second-largest constellation in the sky and contains thousands of galaxies.

Deep-Sky Objects

The Virgo Cluster contains over 1,000 galaxies and forms part of the larger Virgo Supercluster.

Cultural Significance

Virgo is associated with agriculture, justice, and fertility across many cultures.

Deep-Sky Objects

Eridanus

  • Nickname: “The River”
  • Brightest star: Achernar — a hot blue-white star ~139 ly away, one of the flattest known stars due to rapid rotation
  • Location: Southern sky stretching from Orion southward
  • Best months: November-January
  • How to find it: Follow a long winding chain of stars ending at bright Achernar

Background & Mythology

Eridanus was known to ancient Greek astronomers as a celestial river, sometimes linked to the River Nile or the mythological river where Phaethon fell after losing control of the Sun chariot.

Astronomy

Achernar is extremely flattened from spinning rapidly. Eridanus is one of the largest constellations in the sky.

Deep-Sky Objects

The constellation contains the Witch Head Nebula and several nearby galaxies.

Cultural Significance

Its long river shape made it important in many ancient sky traditions and navigation systems.

Hydrus

  • Nickname: “The Water Snake”
  • Brightest star: Beta Hydri — a yellow subgiant ~24 ly away, similar to an older Sun
  • Location: Near the south celestial pole
  • Best months: October-December
  • How to find it: Look below Achernar for a faint zig-zag line of stars

Background & Mythology

Hydrus was created by Dutch navigators in the 1500s to represent a small water snake distinct from the much larger Hydra constellation.

Astronomy

Beta Hydri is one of the closest Sun-like stars and is often studied to understand the Sun's future evolution.

Deep-Sky Objects

Hydrus contains only a few faint galaxies and star systems due to its relatively empty region of sky.

Cultural Significance

Hydrus reflects the Age of Exploration, when European navigators mapped southern skies unseen from Europe.

Background & Mythology

Horologium was created by Nicolas Lacaille in the 18th century and originally represented a pendulum clock.

Astronomy

The constellation is faint but contains several interesting variable stars and galaxies.

Deep-Sky Objects

Horologium contains the Horologium Supercluster, one of the largest known galaxy superclusters.

Cultural Significance

It reflects the scientific instruments and discoveries celebrated during the Enlightenment period.
