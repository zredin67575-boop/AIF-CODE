ANCIENT ASTRONOMY

Long before telescopes, satellites, or computers existed, humans looked up at the night sky and tried to understand what they were seeing. Ancient astronomy developed as people observed the movements of the Sun, Moon, planets, and stars, often using these observations to track time, predict seasons, navigate across large distances, and explain natural phenomena.
Although many ancient civilisations connected astronomy with religion and mythology, their observations were often remarkably accurate. By carefully recording celestial movements over hundreds or even thousands of years, they developed calendars, identified patterns in the sky, and laid the foundations for modern astronomy. Many of the constellations, measurement systems, and astronomical concepts used today can be traced back to ancient cultures.
Ancient astronomy was not developed by a single civilisation. Instead, societies across the world independently studied the sky and created their own methods of understanding it. Some focused on navigation, while others concentrated on agriculture, mathematics, or predicting celestial events. Together, these cultures helped shape humanity's understanding of the universe and influenced the development of modern science.

MESOPOTAMIAN

The Mesopotamians, who lived in the region between the Tigris and Euphrates Rivers (modern-day Iraq), are often considered the founders of scientific astronomy. Beginning around 3000 BCE, Babylonian astronomers carefully recorded the positions of stars, planets, and the Moon on clay tablets.

One of their most important achievements was recognising that celestial events followed predictable patterns. They developed some of the earliest star catalogues and divided the sky into sections that would later influence the modern zodiac. Babylonian astronomers could predict lunar eclipses with surprising accuracy and created mathematical methods for tracking planetary movements.

Their observations became the foundation for later Greek astronomy and influenced many aspects of modern astronomical measurement. The idea that celestial motions could be understood mathematically was one of the most important contributions they made to science

Over 5000 years ago, Babylonians mapped the stars and planets with shocking accuracy.

GREEK

Ancient Greek astronomy transformed observations of the sky into a more scientific discipline. Greek philosophers and astronomers sought explanations for celestial movements using logic, geometry, and mathematics rather than relying solely on mythology.

Astronomers such as Hipparchus created detailed star catalogues and developed the system of stellar magnitudes that is still used today to classify star brightness. Eratosthenes calculated Earth's circumference with remarkable accuracy using observations of shadows, while Aristarchus proposed that the Earth orbited the Sun centuries before this idea became widely accepted.

The Greeks also developed many of the constellations recognised today and introduced mathematical models that influenced astronomy for over a thousand years. Their emphasis on observation, evidence, and reasoning helped establish many of the principles that underpin modern science.

Greek astronomer Hipparchus was famous for creating trigonometry and cataloging the positions of stars.

EGYPTIAN

Ancient Egyptian astronomy was closely connected to religion, agriculture, and the annual flooding of the Nile River. Egyptian astronomers observed the stars to create calendars that helped determine planting and harvesting seasons.

One of their most significant observations involved Sirius, the brightest star in the night sky. The first annual appearance of Sirius just before sunrise, known as its heliacal rising, closely coincided with the flooding of the Nile. This event became one of the most important markers in the Egyptian calendar.

The Egyptians also aligned many structures with astronomical events. The pyramids at Giza are closely aligned with the cardinal directions, and some researchers believe their layout may have been influenced by the stars of Orion's Belt.

Although Egyptian astronomy focused more on practical applications than mathematical models, it contributed significantly to timekeeping, calendars, and astronomical observation.

Ancient Egyptians created temples and pyramids with specific astronomical orientations, showing how astronomy had both religious and practical purposes.

CHINESE

Chinese astronomers created one of the longest continuous records of astronomical observations in human history. For more than 3,000 years, they carefully documented comets, eclipses, planetary movements, and unusual events in the sky.

Chinese astronomers developed detailed star maps and divided the sky into regions different from those used in Western astronomy. They were particularly skilled at recording temporary celestial events, including supernovae. One of the most famous examples is their observation of a supernova in 1054 CE, which created the Crab Nebula that can still be observed today.

Their extensive records provide valuable information that modern astronomers continue to use when studying historical celestial events. The accuracy and consistency of Chinese astronomical observations make them one of the most important sources of ancient astronomical data.

The Dunhuang map is an early star map from China dating to the Tang Dynasty (A.D. 618 to 907).

MESOAMERICAN

Civilisations such as the Maya and Aztec developed some of the most advanced astronomical systems in the ancient world. Their observations focused heavily on tracking the movements of the Sun, Moon, Venus, and other celestial objects.

The Maya created highly accurate calendars based on astronomical cycles. Their calculations of the solar year were remarkably close to modern measurements, and they carefully tracked the movements of Venus because of its cultural and religious importance.

Many Mesoamerican cities and temples were aligned with astronomical events such as solstices and equinoxes. Structures were often designed so that sunlight or shadows created specific effects on important dates.

Their detailed records demonstrate an advanced understanding of celestial cycles and show how astronomy could be integrated into architecture, religion, and daily life.

The Maya standardised the use of caldenders into the modern system.

POLYNESIAN

Unlike many other ancient civilisations, Polynesian astronomy was primarily focused on navigation. Polynesian sailors travelled across vast areas of the Pacific Ocean long before modern navigation technology existed.

They used the positions of stars, the Sun, Moon, ocean currents, wind patterns, and other environmental clues to navigate between islands separated by thousands of kilometres. Expert navigators memorised the rising and setting positions of important stars and used them as guides while travelling.

Polynesian star knowledge allowed people to settle some of the most remote islands on Earth, including Hawaii, New Zealand, and Easter Island. Their navigation techniques remain one of humanity's greatest achievements in practical astronomy.

Today, Polynesian astronomy continues to inspire modern navigation studies and demonstrates how astronomical knowledge can be used for exploration and survival.

Polynesian wayfinders used the stars to navigate their ships at night.
