NORTHERN HEMISPHERE

Most Visible 🌟 🌟 🌟

Ursa Major

  • Nickname: “The Great Bear” / contains the Big Dipper
  • Brightest star: Alioth — a blue-white giant ~81 ly away, magnitude ~1.7
  • Location: Northern sky near Ursa Minor
  • Best months: March-June
  • How to find it: Look for the Big Dipper asterism with four stars forming a bowl and three forming a handle

Background & Mythology

Ursa Major has been recognised by cultures worldwide for thousands of years. In Greek mythology it represents Callisto, transformed into a bear by Hera. Many Indigenous North American cultures also saw it as a great bear being chased across the sky.

Astronomy

The constellation contains many bright stars including Dubhe, Merak, Alioth, and Mizar. The “pointer stars” Dubhe and Merak help locate Polaris in Ursa Minor.

Deep-Sky Objects

Notable objects include Bode's Galaxy (M81), the Cigar Galaxy (M82), and the Pinwheel Galaxy (M101).

Cultural Significance

Ursa Major has been used for navigation for centuries and remains one of the easiest constellations to identify in the Northern Hemisphere.

Ursa Minor

  • Nickname: “The Little Bear”
  • Brightest star: Polaris — a yellow-white supergiant ~433 ly away, the current North Star
  • Location: Near the north celestial pole
  • Best months: Visible all year in northern regions
  • How to find it: Use the Big Dipper pointer stars to trace toward Polaris

Background & Mythology

Ursa Minor was associated with a smaller bear in Greek mythology. Polaris became extremely important in navigation because it remains almost fixed in the sky.

Astronomy

Polaris is actually a multiple star system. The constellation is circumpolar for many northern observers, meaning it never fully sets.

Deep-Sky Objects

Ursa Minor contains fewer deep-sky objects than nearby constellations, but it includes several faint galaxies and nebulae.

Cultural Significance

Ursa Minor and Polaris guided sailors, explorers, and travellers for centuries before modern navigation.

Cassiopeia

  • Nickname: “The Queen”
  • Brightest star: Schedar — an orange giant ~230 ly away
  • Location: Northern sky opposite the Big Dipper
  • Best months: September-January
  • How to find it: Look for a bright “W” or “M” shape of five stars

Background & Mythology

Cassiopeia represents the vain queen from Greek mythology who boasted about her beauty. Zeus placed her in the sky seated upon a throne.

Astronomy

The constellation lies within the Milky Way and contains rich star fields and clusters.

Deep-Sky Objects

Important objects include the Heart Nebula, Soul Nebula, and the open clusters M52 and M103.

Cultural Significance

The W-shape makes Cassiopeia one of the easiest northern constellations to recognise.

Cygnus

  • Nickname: “The Swan” / “Northern Cross”
  • Brightest star: Deneb — a blue-white supergiant ~2,600 ly away
  • Location: Along the Milky Way
  • Best months: June-October
  • How to find it: Look for a large cross shape stretching through the Milky Way

Background & Mythology

Cygnus was associated with a swan in Greek mythology and appears in many cultures worldwide.

Astronomy

Deneb forms the Summer Triangle with Vega and Altair. Cygnus lies in one of the richest regions of the Milky Way.

Deep-Sky Objects

Objects include the North America Nebula, Veil Nebula, and many star-forming regions.

Cultural Significance

Cygnus has been important in mythology, navigation, and seasonal skywatching traditions.

Lyra

  • Nickname: “The Lyre”
  • Brightest star: Vega — a blue-white star ~25 ly away, magnitude ~0.0
  • Location: Northern summer sky
  • Best months: June-September
  • How to find it: Locate the bright star Vega near the Summer Triangle

Background & Mythology

Lyra represents the harp of Orpheus from Greek mythology. Its music was said to charm all living things.

Astronomy

Vega was once the North Star thousands of years ago due to Earth's axial precession.

Deep-Sky Objects

The Ring Nebula (M57), a famous planetary nebula, is located within Lyra.

Cultural Significance

Lyra has long been associated with music, poetry, and mythology.

Aquila

  • Nickname: “The Eagle”
  • Brightest star: Altair — a white main-sequence star ~17 ly away
  • Location: Along the Milky Way
  • Best months: July-September
  • How to find it: Locate Altair in the Summer Triangle with two stars on either side

Background & Mythology

Aquila represents Zeus's eagle in Greek mythology. It appears in many ancient sky traditions.

Astronomy

Altair rotates extremely quickly, causing it to bulge at the equator.

Deep-Sky Objects

The constellation contains open clusters and several planetary nebulae.

Cultural Significance

Aquila is important in seasonal astronomy and forms part of the Summer Triangle.

Andromeda

  • Nickname: “The Chained Princess”
  • Brightest star: Alpheratz — a blue-white binary star ~97 ly away
  • Location: Near Pegasus and Cassiopeia
  • Best months: October-December
  • How to find it: Follow the Great Square of Pegasus outward to a chain of stars

Background & Mythology

Andromeda represents the princess rescued by Perseus in Greek mythology.

Astronomy

The constellation contains the Andromeda Galaxy, the nearest major spiral galaxy to the Milky Way.

Deep-Sky Objects

The Andromeda Galaxy (M31) is visible to the naked eye under dark skies and contains roughly one trillion stars.

Cultural Significance

Andromeda is one of the most historically important constellations in astronomy.

Pegasus

  • Nickname: “The Winged Horse”
  • Brightest star: Enif — an orange supergiant ~690 ly away
  • Location: Northern autumn sky
  • Best months: September-November
  • How to find it: Look for the Great Square of Pegasus

Background & Mythology

Pegasus was the winged horse of Greek mythology, born from Medusa's blood.

Astronomy

The constellation contains many galaxies and exoplanet systems.

Deep-Sky Objects

Objects include Stephan's Quintet and the globular cluster M15.

Cultural Significance

Pegasus has symbolised imagination, flight, and heroism for centuries.

Perseus

  • Nickname: “The Hero”
  • Brightest star: Mirfak — a yellow-white supergiant ~590 ly away
  • Location: Near Andromeda and Cassiopeia
  • Best months: November-February
  • How to find it: Trace outward from Cassiopeia toward a curved chain of stars

Background & Mythology

Perseus represents the Greek hero who defeated Medusa and rescued Andromeda.

Astronomy

The constellation contains Algol, one of the most famous variable stars in the sky.

Deep-Sky Objects

The Double Cluster, NGC 869 and NGC 884, is one of the finest binocular objects in the sky.

Cultural Significance

Perseus is deeply connected to Greek heroic mythology and seasonal northern skies.

Moderately Visible 🌟 🌟

Draco

  • Nickname: “The Dragon”
  • Brightest star: Eltanin — an orange giant star ~150 ly away, magnitude ~2.2
  • Location: Wraps around Ursa Minor in the northern sky
  • Best months: June–September
  • How to find it: Look for a long winding chain of stars curling around the Little Dipper

Background & Mythology

Draco represents the dragon Ladon from Greek mythology, who guarded the golden apples in the Garden of the Hesperides. Hercules later slew the dragon during his twelve labours. Draco was one of the original constellations recorded by the ancient Greeks and appears in many early star catalogues.

Astronomy

Draco is a very large northern constellation that coils around the north celestial pole. Its brightest star, Eltanin, was historically important because it helped James Bradley discover the aberration of starlight in the 1700s.

Deep-Sky Objects

Draco contains the Cat’s Eye Nebula (NGC 6543), one of the most complex planetary nebulae known, as well as the Draco Dwarf Galaxy.

Cultural Significance

Dragons appear in myths worldwide, and Draco has often symbolised guardianship, danger, or cosmic power across many cultures.

Cepheus

  • Nickname: “The King”
  • Brightest star: Alderamin — a white giant star ~49 ly away, magnitude ~2.5
  • Location: Near Cassiopeia and Draco
  • Best months: August–November
  • How to find it: Look for a house-shaped pentagon near Cassiopeia’s “W” shape

Background & Mythology

Cepheus was the king of Ethiopia in Greek mythology and husband of Cassiopeia. He was the father of Andromeda and forms part of the famous Perseus myth family placed together in the night sky.

Astronomy

The constellation contains Delta Cephei, the prototype Cepheid variable star. Cepheid variables are extremely important in astronomy because their brightness changes can be used to measure distances across galaxies.

Deep-Sky Objects

Cepheus contains the Wizard Nebula, the Iris Nebula, and several rich open star clusters along the Milky Way.

Cultural Significance

Cepheid variable stars helped astronomers discover the true scale of the universe and measure distances to faraway galaxies.

Boötes

  • Nickname: “The Herdsman”
  • Brightest star: Arcturus — an orange giant ~37 ly away, magnitude ~−0.05
  • Location: Near Ursa Major
  • Best months: April–July
  • How to find it: Follow the curve of the Big Dipper’s handle to Arcturus

Background & Mythology

Boötes is usually associated with a herdsman or ploughman in Greek mythology. Some myths identify him as Icarius, a man taught winemaking by Dionysus.

Astronomy

Arcturus is one of the brightest stars in Earth’s night sky and is much larger and older than the Sun. Boötes is also home to several binary and variable stars.

Deep-Sky Objects

The constellation contains many distant galaxies and the Boötes Void — one of the largest known empty regions of space.

Cultural Significance

Arcturus has been used for navigation and seasonal timing in cultures around the world for thousands of years.

Hercules

  • Nickname: “The Hero”
  • Brightest star: Kornephoros — a yellow giant ~140 ly away
  • Location: Between Lyra and Boötes
  • Best months: June–August
  • How to find it: Look for the “Keystone” shape in the centre of the constellation

Background & Mythology

Hercules represents the legendary Greek hero famous for completing the twelve labours. The constellation has ancient origins dating back to Babylonian astronomy.

Astronomy

Although Hercules is large, many of its stars are relatively faint. The constellation sits high in northern summer skies.

Deep-Sky Objects

Hercules contains the Great Hercules Cluster (M13), one of the brightest and most famous globular clusters visible from Earth.

Cultural Significance

Hercules symbolises strength, endurance, and heroism in many retellings of Greek mythology.

Corona Borealis

  • Nickname: “The Northern Crown”
  • Brightest star: Alphecca — a white binary star ~75 ly away
  • Location: Between Boötes and Hercules
  • Best months: May–July
  • How to find it: Look for a semicircle or crown-shaped arc of stars

Background & Mythology

This constellation represents the crown given to Ariadne by Dionysus in Greek mythology. The crown was later placed into the heavens.

Astronomy

Corona Borealis is small but easy to recognise because of its neat curved shape.

Deep-Sky Objects

The constellation contains the Corona Borealis Galaxy Cluster, a massive grouping of distant galaxies.

Cultural Significance

Crowns and circular star patterns have symbolised royalty and divinity in many ancient cultures.

Coma Berenices

  • Nickname: “Berenice’s Hair”
  • Brightest star: Beta Comae Berenices — a Sun-like star ~30 ly away
  • Location: Near Leo and Virgo
  • Best months: April–June
  • How to find it: Look for a faint fuzzy patch of stars between Leo and Boötes

Background & Mythology

The constellation is named after Queen Berenice II of Egypt, who sacrificed her hair to the gods for her husband’s safe return from war.

Astronomy

Coma Berenices contains many faint stars and lies far from the Milky Way plane, making distant galaxies easier to observe.

Deep-Sky Objects

The constellation contains the Coma Star Cluster and the huge Coma Galaxy Cluster.

Cultural Significance

It is one of the few constellations directly named after a historical person rather than mythology.

Delphinus

  • Nickname: “The Dolphin”
  • Brightest star: Rotanev — a blue-white giant ~97 ly away
  • Location: Near Aquila and Pegasus
  • Best months: July–September
  • How to find it: Look for a small diamond-shaped pattern with a tail

Background & Mythology

In Greek mythology, Delphinus represents a dolphin sent by Poseidon. Dolphins were seen as intelligent and sacred creatures by ancient sailors.

Astronomy

Although small, Delphinus is relatively easy to identify because of its compact and distinctive shape.

Deep-Sky Objects

Delphinus contains several faint globular clusters and planetary nebulae.

Cultural Significance

Dolphins appear in myths and navigation traditions throughout Mediterranean and Polynesian cultures.

Sagitta

  • Nickname: “The Arrow”
  • Brightest star: Gamma Sagittae — a red giant ~275 ly away, magnitude ~3.5
  • Location: Between Aquila and Cygnus
  • Best months: July–September
  • How to find it: Look for a small arrow-shaped line of stars near the Summer Triangle

Background & Mythology

Sagitta is one of the oldest constellations, appearing in Greek and Roman astronomy. It represents an arrow shot by Hercules or Apollo depending on the myth.

Astronomy

Sagitta is small but easy to recognise due to its arrow shape. It lies within a rich Milky Way region.

Deep-Sky Objects

The constellation contains the globular cluster Messier 71, once thought to be an open cluster due to its loose structure.

Cultural Significance

Sagitta symbolises weapons, hunting, and heroic myths in many ancient sky traditions.

Vulpecula

  • Nickname: “The Little Fox”
  • Brightest star: Anser — a red giant ~300 ly away, magnitude ~4.4
  • Location: Near Cygnus in the Milky Way
  • Best months: July–October
  • How to find it: Look between Cygnus and Sagitta in a dense Milky Way region

Background & Mythology

Vulpecula was introduced by Johannes Hevelius in the 1600s and originally represented a fox carrying a goose.

Astronomy

The constellation is faint but sits directly within the Milky Way, making it rich in stars.

Deep-Sky Objects

Vulpecula contains the Dumbbell Nebula (M27), the first planetary nebula ever discovered.

Cultural Significance

Although modern rather than ancient, Vulpecula reflects the trend of naming constellations after animals and tools during the Age of Exploration.

Lacerta

  • Nickname: “The Lizard”
  • Brightest star: Alpha Lacertae — a white main-sequence star ~100 ly away, magnitude ~3.8
  • Location: Between Cygnus and Andromeda
  • Best months: August–October
  • How to find it: Look for a faint zig-zag chain of stars between Cygnus and Cassiopeia

Background & Mythology

Lacerta was created by Johannes Hevelius in the 17th century and has no major mythology attached to it.

Astronomy

The constellation is faint and mainly composed of dim stars, but lies near rich Milky Way regions.

Deep-Sky Objects

Lacerta contains the open cluster NGC 7243 and the BL Lacertae object, a powerful active galactic nucleus.

Cultural Significance

Lacerta represents the scientific naming traditions of early modern astronomers.

Auriga

  • Nickname: “The Charioteer”
  • Brightest star: Capella — a yellow giant system ~43 ly away, magnitude ~0.1
  • Location: Northern winter sky near Taurus and Gemini
  • Best months: December–February
  • How to find it: Look for a large pentagon shape with bright Capella at one corner

Background & Mythology

Auriga is associated with Erichthonius, a legendary Greek charioteer and inventor of the four-horse chariot.

Astronomy

Capella is one of the brightest stars in the night sky and is actually a multiple-star system.

Deep-Sky Objects

Auriga contains three bright open clusters: M36, M37, and M38, all visible with binoculars.

Cultural Significance

Auriga has appeared in Greek, Roman, and Arabic astronomy for thousands of years.

Canes Venatici

  • Nickname: “The Hunting Dogs”
  • Brightest star: Cor Caroli — a binary system ~110 ly away, magnitude ~2.8
  • Location: Near Ursa Major and Boötes
  • Best months: March–June
  • How to find it: Look below the handle of the Big Dipper

Background & Mythology

Created by Johannes Hevelius, the constellation represents the hunting dogs of Boötes.

Astronomy

Cor Caroli is one of the most beautiful binary stars visible in small telescopes.

Deep-Sky Objects

Canes Venatici contains the Whirlpool Galaxy (M51), one of the most famous spiral galaxies in the sky.

Cultural Significance

The constellation reflects hunting themes common in European astronomy traditions.

Lynx

  • Nickname: “The Lynx”
  • Brightest star: Alpha Lyncis — an orange giant ~200 ly away, magnitude ~3.1
  • Location: Between Ursa Major and Auriga
  • Best months: January–April
  • How to find it: Look for a faint zig-zag chain of stars near Ursa Major

Background & Mythology

Lynx was introduced by Hevelius, who joked that only someone with the eyes of a lynx could see it clearly.

Astronomy

The constellation is made mostly of faint stars and covers a large area of the sky.

Deep-Sky Objects

Lynx contains several distant galaxies and galaxy groups observable with larger telescopes.

Cultural Significance

Lynx represents the expansion of constellation mapping during the scientific revolution.

Camelopardalis

  • Nickname: “The Giraffe”
  • Brightest star: Beta Camelopardalis — a yellow supergiant ~1,000 ly away, magnitude ~4.0
  • Location: Near Ursa Minor and Cassiopeia
  • Best months: December–March
  • How to find it: Look for a large faint region between Ursa Major and Cassiopeia

Background & Mythology

Camelopardalis was created in the 1600s and named after the giraffe, once believed to resemble a camel-leopard hybrid.

Astronomy

The constellation is large but faint, making it difficult to identify in bright skies.

Deep-Sky Objects

Camelopardalis contains the Kemble Cascade, a beautiful line of stars visible with binoculars.

Cultural Significance

It reflects the influence of exploration and newly discovered animals on constellation naming.

Background & Mythology

Sextans was introduced by Johannes Hevelius to honour the sextant astronomy instrument.

Deep-Sky Objects

Sextans contains several distant galaxies and galaxy clusters.

Cultural Significance

Sextans reflects the rise of scientific instruments during the Age of Astronomy.

  • Location: Between Orion and Canis Major
  • Best months: January–March

Background & Mythology

Monoceros was introduced in the 1600s and represents the mythical unicorn.

Astronomy

The constellation lies directly within the Milky Way and contains many star-forming regions.

Deep-Sky Objects

Monoceros contains the Rosette Nebula, Christmas Tree Cluster, and Cone Nebula.

Cultural Significance

Monoceros reflects mythical creatures and imagination in constellation naming traditions.

Cancer

  • Nickname: “The Crab” (Zodiac)
  • Brightest star: Beta Cancri (Altarf) — an orange giant ~290 ly away, magnitude ~3.5
  • Location: Between Gemini and Leo
  • Best months: February–April
  • How to find it: Look for a faint upside-down Y shape between Gemini and Leo

Background & Mythology

In Greek mythology, Cancer represents the crab sent by Hera to distract Hercules during his battle with the Hydra.

Astronomy

Cancer is relatively faint but lies along the zodiac, the path followed by the Sun, Moon, and planets.

Deep-Sky Objects

The constellation contains the Beehive Cluster (M44), one of the closest and brightest open clusters in the sky.

Cultural Significance

Cancer has appeared in Babylonian, Greek, and Roman astronomy for thousands of years.

Leo Minor

  • Nickname: “The Smaller Lion”
  • Brightest star: Praecipua — a yellow giant ~98 ly away, magnitude ~3.8
  • Location: Between Leo and Ursa Major
  • Best months: March–May
  • How to find it: Look for a faint triangle of stars above Leo

Background & Mythology

Leo Minor was introduced by Johannes Hevelius in the 17th century to fill a gap between larger constellations.

Astronomy

The constellation is faint and contains mainly dim stars.

Deep-Sky Objects

Leo Minor contains several distant galaxies observable with medium to large telescopes.

Cultural Significance

It reflects the expansion of constellation mapping during the scientific revolution.

Triangulum

  • Nickname: “The Triangle”
  • Brightest star: Beta Trianguli — a white star system ~125 ly away, magnitude ~3.0
  • Location: Near Andromeda and Aries
  • Best months: October–December
  • How to find it: Look for a small thin triangle near Andromeda

Background & Mythology

Triangulum is one of the oldest known constellations and was recognised by the Greeks and Babylonians.

Astronomy

The constellation is small but easy to identify due to its simple geometric shape.

Deep-Sky Objects

Triangulum contains the Triangulum Galaxy (M33), one of the closest spiral galaxies to the Milky Way.

Cultural Significance

The constellation’s triangular shape made it important in ancient geometry and astronomy.

Aries

  • Nickname: “The Ram” (Zodiac)
  • Brightest star: Hamal — an orange giant ~66 ly away, magnitude ~2.0
  • Location: Between Pisces and Taurus
  • Best months: October–December
  • How to find it: Look for a short curved line of three bright stars

Background & Mythology

Aries represents the golden ram from Greek mythology whose fleece became the Golden Fleece sought by Jason and the Argonauts.

Astronomy

Aries lies along the zodiac and contains several bright nearby stars.

Deep-Sky Objects

The constellation contains several faint galaxies and star systems observable with telescopes.

Cultural Significance

Aries was historically important because the Sun once crossed the vernal equinox within it.

Pisces

  • Location: Between Aquarius and Aries

  • How to find it: Look for two faint loops connected by a long line

Background & Mythology

Pisces represents two fish tied together in Greek mythology, linked to Aphrodite and Eros escaping a monster.

Astronomy

The constellation is large but faint and lies along the zodiac.

Deep-Sky Objects

Pisces contains the spiral galaxy M74, known for its well-defined spiral arms.

Cultural Significance

Pisces has appeared in Babylonian and Greek astronomy for over two thousand years.

Background & Mythology

Aquarius is linked to Ganymede, the cupbearer of the gods in Greek mythology.

Astronomy

The constellation is large and faint but important due to its zodiac position.

Deep-Sky Objects

Aquarius contains the Helix Nebula, one of the closest planetary nebulae to Earth.

Cultural Significance

Aquarius has long been associated with water, floods, and seasonal rains in ancient cultures.

Background & Mythology

Capricornus represents the mythical sea-goat associated with the Greek god Pan.

Astronomy

Cultural Significance

The constellation has roots in Babylonian astronomy dating back thousands of years.

Background & Mythology

Equuleus is one of the smallest and oldest constellations in the sky.

Equuleus contains few major deep-sky objects due to its small size.

Ophiuchus

  • Location: Above Scorpius and Sagittarius

Background & Mythology

Astronomy

The constellation lies across the Milky Way and is rich in star fields and clusters.

Deep-Sky Objects

Ophiuchus contains many globular clusters and Barnard’s Star, one of the nearest stars to Earth.

Cultural Significance

Ophiuchus is sometimes called the “13th zodiac constellation” because the Sun passes through it.

Serpens

  • Nickname: “The Serpent”
  • Brightest star: Unukalhai — an orange giant ~74 ly away, magnitude ~2.6
  • Location: Split around Ophiuchus
  • Best months: June–August
  • How to find it: Look for two separate star chains surrounding Ophiuchus

Background & Mythology

Serpens is the only constellation officially divided into two sections: Serpens Caput and Serpens Cauda.

Astronomy

The constellation stretches across rich Milky Way regions filled with stars and nebulae.

Deep-Sky Objects

Serpens contains the Eagle Nebula (M16), home to the famous “Pillars of Creation.”

Cultural Significance

Snakes symbolised healing, wisdom, and rebirth in many ancient cultures.

  • Best months: June–August
  • How to find it: Look for a faint patch within the Milky Way below Aquila

Background & Mythology

Scutum was created by Johannes Hevelius in the 1600s and named after a Polish king’s shield.

Astronomy

The constellation lies directly within the Milky Way and contains dense star fields.

Deep-Sky Objects

Scutum contains the Wild Duck Cluster (M11), one of the richest open clusters visible from Earth.

Cultural Significance

Scutum reflects the tradition of commemorating historical figures through astronomy.
