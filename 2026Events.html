WHAT IS AN ECLIPSE?

An eclipse is an astronomical event occuring when one celestial object, such as a moon or planet, moves into the shadow of another object or has another object pass between it and the viewer. This creates a syzygy, or alignment of three objects. The two main types of eclipses are solar - Moon blocks Sun - and lunar - Earth blocks Sun from Moon.

WHAT IS A PLANET PARADE?

A planet parade is a rare astronomical event where multiple planets in our solar system are visible in the same area of the sky as seen from Earth. It only seems as though the planets are 'lining up' due to our perspective.

To see this year's planet parade, look to the western sky 30-90 minutes after sunset. Mercury, Venus, Jupiter, and Saturn can ususally be seen with the naked eye, but you may need binoculars or a telescope to see Uranus and Neptune.

LUNAR OCCULTATIONS

Lunar occultations occur when the moon passes infront of a celestial object such as a star, planet, or asteroid, temporarily hiding it from view. They're a bit like a localised miniature eclipse. Any occultation occurs when a body with a greater apparent diameter (how big it is from our perspective) passes in front of a body with a smaller apparent diameter. If the nearer object is smaller, it is called a mutual transit.

    • LUNAR PHASES

    The lunar cycle occurs approximately every 29.5 days and follows a specific cycle with different phases. Key words to know when understanding the lunar cycle are gibbous, crescent, waxing, waning, new/full, and quarter. Gibbous refers to more than one half of the moon being illuminated, and crescent to less than one half. Waxing means getting bigger, and waning getting smaller. The reason the 'middle' phases are called first/third quarters, are because we only see one side of the moon, meaning only a quarter of the moon is illuminated.

    THE MOON, TIDES, AND GRAVITY

    The Moon drives tides through its gravitational pull, which stretches Earth's oceans into a "bulge" on both the near and far sides, creating two high tides and two low tides daily. The Moon's phases affect tide magnitude: new and full moons produce maximum spring tides (high high tides) due to aligned solar/lunar gravity, while quarter moons produce moderate neap tides.

    When the Moon is closest to Earth (perigee), it exerts a stronger pull, resulting in higher-than-average tides known as perigean spring tides, often occurring 6-8 times a year.

    Because the moon orbits the Earth, meaning its relative position changes, the high/low tides occur around 50 minutes later each day.

    LUNAR OBJECTS

    Natural lunar objects consist of diverse geographical features and materials resulting from millions of years of cosmic and volcanic activity.

    Some key formations include:

  • South Pole-Atiken (SPA) Basin: one of the largest and oldest impact craters in the solar system, spanning the far side of the moon.
  • Mare Imbrium (Sea of Rains): A massive lava-filled basin in the northern hemisphere.
    • Map of seas on the near side of the moon.
  • Oceanus Procellarum (Ocean of Storms): The largest dark lunar "sea" (maria) located on the western side of the near side.
  • Aristarchus Crater: A bright, prominent crater located near the Bay of Rainbows, easily visible with binoculars.
  • Vallis Schröteri (Schröter's Valley): A sinuous rille (lava valley) near the Aristarchus crater.
  • Reiner Gamma: A notable "lunar swirl"—a high-albedo (bright) feature located in a region with a magnetic field.
    • Reiner Gamma.
  • Catena Mendeleev: A chain of craters within the Mendeleev crater
