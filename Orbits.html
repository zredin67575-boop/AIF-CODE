ORBITS

WHAT IS AN ORBIT?

Put simply, an orbit is the gravitationally curved trajectory of an object around a point in space. In celestial mechanics, it is fundamentally an ongoing state of free form.

HOW CAN PHYSICS EXPLAIN ORBITS?

GRAVITY:

This amazing visible force between two masses acts as an inwardly anchor continually tugging the orbiting body such as a planet or satellite toward the center of mass of the larger central body.

CENTRIPETAL FORCE:

Centripetal force is the label given to any net force that keeps an object moving in a curved path. In orbital mechanics, gravity provides this force. Because it is directed towards the center, it continually changes the object's direction without changing its speed.

CIRCULAR VELOCITY:

Circular velocity or orbital speed is the precise tangential speed an object must maintain to stay in perfectly circular orbit. To maintain a specific orbit, an object's forward speed must match the pull of gravity. If it travels too slowly, it is pulled into a downward spiral by gravity. If it is too fast, it will break free or move in a large orbit. This velocity depends on the distance between two bodies and the mass of the primary object

ELLIPSES AND CIRCULAR ORBITS:

While circular orbits are used for simple physics calculations, most real-world orbits are ellipses or squash-stretched circles. A circular orbit has an eccentricity of zero, whilst elliptical orbits have an eccentricity between zero and one. Real orbits rarely have a perfectly balanced speed. When a body moves closer to the central mass, the gravitational pull strengthens, speeding the object up. As it swings further away, gravity weakens and the object slows down. In an ellipse, the central body sits at one of the two foci inside the oval rather than the exact center.

HOW IS OUR UNIVERSE MADE OF ORBITS?

Orbits shape the universe through gravity and momentum. At a local level, planets, dwarf planets, asteroids and comets orbit the sun in elliptical orbits. Because every celestial body possesses mass, they do not simply revolve around the exact centre of our sun. Instead, objects and the sun orbit the common centre of mass known as the barycenter.

Just as planets orbit stars, smaller bodies orbit planets. Natural and artificial satellites are held in continuous orbit by the gravitational pull of the planet they circle.

On a greater scale, billions of stars, along with vast clouds of gas and dark matter, are bound together into galaxies. In the Milky Way, our solar system orbits Sagittarius A star around 830,000 kilometres per hour, taking around 230 million years to complete a single orbit.

SUBATOMIC PARTICLES:

Orbits don't just happen in deep space. At a microscopic level, the fundamental building blocks of matter, atoms, operate similarly. Negatively charged electrons orbit the dense positively charged nucleus in complex patterns governed by quantum mechanics

HOW DO ORBITS HELP ASTRONOMERS FIND DARK MATTER AND ENERGY?

Dark matter and energy are unknown and unobservable objects in our universe. Astronomers and physicists have long pondered over what they may be made up of and what they can teach us about quantum physics. As dark matter and energy make up around 95% of our universe, many objects orbit around it.

When astronomers notice a star or planet orbiting around a blank or dark spot in space, they know that there must be something there. By pointing telescopes at it and tracking star and planet motions around dark matter, they can determine how large the object is and can potentially observe an event horizon around it. In this case, it would be a black hole, and the event horizon is the place where light cannot escape, meaning there'll be a dark void in the middle of the astronomer's observation.
